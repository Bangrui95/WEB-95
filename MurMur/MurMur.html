Standardised Power

MurMur Radio examines systems of surveillance and collective control through the language of modular installation. The project draws upon the logic of standardisation, integration, and centralised broadcasting — structures that appear to generate order and efficiency, yet quietly shape behaviour and direct the flow of information.

The installation is composed of interchangeable modules, each resembling simplified architectural or industrial units. These components can be rearranged and recombined, forming shifting structural relationships. This modular organisation mirrors contemporary systems that segment, categorise, and integrate individuals into unified networks. Each unit appears autonomous, yet remains connected to a central mechanism of control and transmission. The modular form thus becomes both a formal strategy and a metaphor for governance.

Participants interact with living plants embedded within the system. By touching the plants, biological signals are activated, captured, and processed before being transmitted to a central broadcasting structure. The system converts these signals into a low, continuous murmur — a dispersed yet collectively amplified sound. Information moves from body to system, from organism to broadcast, transforming individual expression into mediated transmission.

"Pirate Radio"

The installation constructs a tension between individual signal and centralised amplification. It reflects on how voices are collected, translated, and redistributed within controlled infrastructures. By combining modular design with distributed sound, MurMur Radio shifts attention from surveillance as mere observation to the structural pathways through which information is organised, integrated, and made audible.
